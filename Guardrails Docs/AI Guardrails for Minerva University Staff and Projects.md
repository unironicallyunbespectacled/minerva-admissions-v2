**Purpose**: This document is meant to support the use of AI by Minerva University staff and for administrative projects in ways that adhere to both institutional values and prioritize outcomes that value human contribution and decision-making. It is meant as a set of guardrails for both individual use with AI chatbots as well as ways of scoping projects that utilize AI for efficiency, effectiveness, and acceleration of human approaches to problem-solving.

**Non-Purpose**: This document is not meant to be prescriptive or a rigid set of rules that must be followed each time a staff member uses AI or scopes a project that leverages LLMs or the larger concept of machine learning. Rather, it is merely an attempt to ensure that we do not substitute computational power for critical thinking.

**Scope and Applicability:** This document applies to Minerva University staff and staff-led administrative or institutional projects that make use of AI systems, including but not limited to LLMs, ML systems, and automated decision-support tools.

**Guardrails Key Considerations and Questions**:  
*Each AI-enabled project must explicitly document (\#processdocumentation) responses to the following considerations prior to deployment, so that it can be referenced later as well in the case of violation of any of these guardrails.*

* Copyright, Intellectual Property, and Data Privacy:   
  * Who owns the training data? The AI model? The process built to leverage the AI? The product/outcomes/result of the AI?  
  * How has institutional data and student data privacy been protected within this use of AI?  
* Disclosure:  
  * Who needs to be informed about the use of AI within the project or by the individual?  
  * How has the use of AI been disclosed within the outcome/product?  
  * Have the stakeholders been given the opportunity to opt-out/opt-in of the use of AI before, during, or after the project/work has been completed?  
* Decision-making/Responsibility:  
  * As computers can never be held accountable for decisions made with their help, who is the responsible party for any impact of the decisions made with the use of AI?  
  * What is the decision-making structure/process that prioritizes human evaluation of AI products/outputs (success criteria, feedback, iteration)?  
* Bias and Trust:  
  * What measures have been taken to remove biased training data from the AI model?  
  * What level of confidence/trust do the stakeholders have that decisions/outcomes based upon AI work will be free of bias?  
* Values:  
  * What explicit values have been stated with which this project or work with AI should align?  
  * What would constitute a breach of these values such that the use of AI should be rethought or entirely abandoned?  
* Other Important Considerations:  
  * Vendor/Third-Party Usage and Agreements  
  * Accessibility and Equity of Access  
  * Documentation, Performance Monitoring, and Quality Assurance  
  * AI Competency and Training (for the humans)  
  * Environmental and Resource Sustainability

**Example of Guardrails Outline (for Admissions Projects)**:

* Copyright, Intellectual Property, and Data Privacy:  
  * Data used: Applicant data submitted by prospective Minerva University students  
  * Data ownership: All data is owned by Minerva University for specific evaluative use and is not to be used for training any models outside the control of Minerva University (publicly available models)  
* Disclosure:  
  * Applicants should be told that computational/machine learning tools are used in the evaluation of their materials both prior to submitting and after decisions have been reached, but that humans were responsible for the decisions and the methodology  
  * By submitting materials to the university, the applicants will opt-in to the use of computational tools to support efficient and effective evaluation of their application.  
    * By submitting applications and opting in to AI evaluation of applications, does the opting in end at just evaluation or it also includes opting in to training future models?  
* Decision-making/Responsibility:  
  * The Admissions team at Minerva University is responsible for all use of AI. Admissions decisions are a joint responsibility between academic, admissions, administrative, and financial aid teams.  
  * All uses of AI have been vetted and approved by Admissions staff in consultation with both Faculty and (current) Student stakeholders  
  * When an AI system performs evaluations but cannot review the full application cycle, should its assessment be regarded as comparable to that produced by a human evaluator? Additionally, do both forms of evaluation provide an equal level of competency for informing sound decision-making by the appropriate parties?  
* Bias/Trust:  
  * Application data for use in training models/methods have been selected to eliminate bias and to preserve fidelity of evaluation to preserve human-evaluated standards.  
  * Each evaluation process managed by an AI will be given a confidence score for adherence to standards established by human reviewers.  
    * There should be a threshold that if a score is consistently wanting, there  is a system override till everything is resolved.  
  * In the same way that multiple human evaluators review the same material to mitigate individual bias, multiple AI evaluations, either through repeated runs or parallel models, should be employed to reduce model-specific bias.  
* Values:  
  * Empathy, Fairness, Trust, Accountability, Privacy, Care  
  * In the embodiment of transparency, there should be a constitution that is readable to humans and AI models that gives accurate and precise guidelines to the AI. The constitution, like Anthropic’s, could even be made public and open source to allow continuous exchange of ideas.([https://www.anthropic.com/constitution?](https://www.anthropic.com/constitution))  
* Documentation, Performance Monitoring, and Quality Assurance  
  * Rigorous performance evaluations after given time periods should be conducted to evaluate the current status of the evaluation system.

**PART 2: Proposed AI Guardrails Framework for Students**

# **2.1  Purpose**

This framework establishes guardrails for student use of artificial intelligence tools across all academic and professional contexts at Minerva University. It operationalizes Minerva's existing AI Policy by providing students with structured, context-specific guidance that supports principled decision-making.

These guardrails are not prohibitions. They are structured boundaries that preserve what matters most: the integrity of your thinking, the value of your learning, the protection of private information, and your accountability as the author of your work. AI tools, used well, are powerful partners for learning. Used without structure, they undermine the very capabilities Minerva is built to develop.

# **2.2  Core Values**

All student AI use at Minerva must be guided by the following values:

| Value | What This Means for Students |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Human-Centered Learning** | AI is a tool that supports your thinking. It does not replace it. You remain the author, the analyst, and the learner. |
| **Accountability** | You are fully responsible for every submission — its accuracy, its tone, its integrity — regardless of what AI generated. |
| **Transparency** | Disclose AI use honestly, completely, and in the required format. Concealment is a breach of the honor code. |
| **Privacy and Data Protection** | Protect confidential information: Minerva's, your classmates', and your employer's. Know the terms of the AI tools you use. |
| **Intellectual Property** | Respect copyright in AI inputs and outputs. Understand the IP implications of AI-assisted work you produce. |
| **Fairness and Equity** | Do not use AI in ways that create unfair advantages over peers who are not using it, or that undermine assessment validity. |
| **Educational Value** | If AI use for a task reduces your learning rather than enhancing it, that use conflicts with your purpose at Minerva. |
| **Care** | Consider the downstream impact of your AI-assisted work on classmates, communities, and others affected by it. |

# **2.3  Scope**

This framework applies to all Minerva University enrolled students and covers the following contexts:

* Coursework: All assignments, papers, projects, presentations, polls, and pre-class work submitted for any Minerva course.

* Research: Independent and faculty-mentored research, literature reviews, data analysis, and academic writing.

* Internships and Professional Work: AI use in deliverables produced for external employers, clients, or organizations as part of Minerva-affiliated internship programs.

* Independent Projects: Work produced outside formal coursework, including startups, creative projects, and self-directed learning.

* Student Organizations: AI use in communications, content, and materials produced by Minerva student organizations.

* External Collaborations: Partnerships with external organizations, competitions, or community projects affiliated with Minerva.

This framework does not supersede course-specific AI policies established by individual faculty. Where instructor policies are more restrictive than this framework, instructor policies govern. Where they are silent, this framework provides the default.

# **2.4  Guardrails by Context**

## **2.4.1  Learning and Coursework**

The single most important principle for AI use in coursework: AI is a thinking partner, not a thinking substitute. The purpose of your Minerva education is to develop transferable competencies through active intellectual engagement. Any use of AI that short-circuits that engagement, even if the resulting work is technically compliant, undermines your education.

### **Permitted Uses (unless restricted by your instructor)**

* Brainstorming and idea generation after you have engaged independently with the material

* Outlining (with your own content, refined through AI dialogue)

* Editing and proofreading (with full revision and ownership of changes)

* Coding support, debugging, and algorithm exploration (with understanding of all submitted code)

* Tutoring: using AI to quiz you, challenge your assumptions, or explain concepts

* Research support: using AI to suggest sources, then independently verifying and obtaining them

* Accessibility support: speech-to-text, text simplification, diagram generation for learning differences

* Interview and presentation practice using AI voice modes

### **Risky Uses — Proceed with Caution**

* Using AI-generated summaries as your primary or only engagement with assigned readings

* Using AI to complete practice exercises you haven't attempted independently first

* Using AI early in a research process before forming your own initial position

* Relying on AI for any task where your undeveloped skill is the intended learning outcome

### **Prohibited Uses**

* Generating full sections, paragraphs, or arguments for submission in your name

* Using AI to complete in-class polls, pre-class work, or verbal contributions

* Using AI for any assessment specifically prohibited by your instructor

* Submitting AI-assisted work without required disclosure

* Representing AI-generated content as your own original thinking

| Guiding Question for Coursework Before using AI for any coursework task, ask: "If I use AI for this, am I gaining a skill or avoiding developing one?" If the answer is the latter, that use is risky or prohibited regardless of technical permissibility. |
| :---- |

## **2.4.2  Disclosure and Transparency**

Disclosure of AI use is mandatory whenever AI tools have materially contributed to a submitted work product. Disclosure is an act of intellectual honesty and a requirement of the honor code.

### **Required Disclosure Format**

Unless your instructor specifies otherwise, include a completed Acknowledgments section in every submission where AI was used. The Acknowledgments section must include, for each AI tool used:

1. Tool name and version or date (e.g., Claude Sonnet, March 2026\)

2. Purpose in your workflow (e.g., brainstorming, outlining, debugging, editing)

3. A shareable link to the full chat session showing all prompts and outputs

4. How you modified, verified, or discarded the AI output

5. A brief reflection on what value the AI use added to your learning or work quality

For internship and external project work, disclosure norms are governed by your employer's policies. However, you should never represent AI-assisted work as entirely your own to an employer without disclosure. When employer policies are silent, disclose.

| Disclosure Threshold Ask yourself: Would a reader of my work, knowing exactly how AI contributed to it, feel I had been honest with them? If not, your disclosure is incomplete. |
| :---- |

## **2.4.3  Confidentiality**

Public AI systems (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and others) process data on external servers. Entering confidential information into these systems constitutes sharing it with a third party and may constitute a breach of institutional, legal, or professional obligations.

### **You must not upload to public AI systems:**

* Unpublished Minerva course materials, syllabi, or proprietary academic content unless you have verified this is permitted

* Any information about identifiable individuals (classmates, peers, clients, employees) without their knowledge and consent

* Proprietary or confidential employer, client, or partner information from internships or external collaborations

* Any data protected by institutional data agreements, NDA, or privacy law

* Student records, financial information, or other data protected by FERPA or applicable privacy frameworks

### **Before inputting any material to an AI system, ask:**

* Is this information confidential, proprietary, or subject to privacy protections?

* Does the AI tool's privacy policy allow it to use my inputs for model training?

* Would the author, institution, or subject of this information consent to it being shared with this system?

If you are uncertain about any of these questions, do not input the material until you have verified the answer.

## **2.4.4  Data Privacy**

You are responsible for understanding the terms of service and data practices of any AI tool you use. Key questions to research before using a new AI tool:

* Does this tool use my inputs or conversation history to train its models? If yes, treat it as a public system.

* Is there an education or enterprise version with stronger data protections?

* What data is retained, for how long, and who can access it?

Practical guidelines:

* Use AI tools with active data protection settings where available (e.g., disabling chat history in ChatGPT, using Anthropic's privacy settings in Claude)

* For any assignment involving real identifiable people, anonymize before using AI assistance

* Never photograph or scan identifiable personal documents and submit to AI systems

* Report any suspected AI tool data breach or misuse to your instructor and the relevant Minerva administrative contact

## **2.4.5  Intellectual Property**

AI use raises important questions about who owns what. Minerva students should understand the following baseline principles:

### **Ownership of AI-Assisted Work**

* Work you produce using AI as a tool, where your intellectual contribution is substantial and AI serves a supporting role, is generally yours. The more AI generates independently, the weaker your ownership claim.

* If you are producing work for a course, any IP policies in the course syllabus govern. If silent, standard academic IP norms apply.

* If you are producing work during an internship using employer resources or AI tools, the employer likely owns that work. Clarify with your supervisor.

### **Copyright in AI Inputs**

* Do not submit copyrighted materials to AI systems for analysis or transformation without verifying this is legally and contractually permissible.

* Do not use AI to reproduce substantial portions of copyrighted text, music, art, or code without authorization.

### **AI-Generated Content**

* AI-generated text, code, and images may have complex or unclear copyright status. Do not submit AI-generated content as your own original creative work without appropriate disclosure.

* Do not use AI to replicate the distinctive style of specific living creators in ways that could constitute misappropriation.

## **2.4.6  Responsible AI Use and Critical Thinking**

The most significant risk of AI use in education is not plagiarism; it is the gradual depletion of your ability to think independently when AI thinking becomes a substitute for your own. Research from MIT (Kosmyna et al., 2025\) demonstrates that relying on AI for essay writing reduces memory formation, attention, and perceived authorship. The Minerva competency model is designed to build the very capacities most threatened by uncritical AI use.

Guidelines for responsible AI use that protects your cognitive development:

* Engage with primary sources, readings, and problems independently before turning to AI

* Use AI in dialogue: ask it to challenge your ideas, not give you ideas

* Apply Anthropic's Discernment principle: Regularly ask yourself whether you are genuinely learning or merely producing output

* Maintain the ability to explain, defend, and stand behind every element of your submitted work

* Treat AI outputs as a first draft requiring substantial intellectual engagement, not a final product

| The Meno Principle To effectively use AI, to prompt it well and evaluate its outputs critically, you must already have meaningful domain knowledge. This is why AI use cannot replace fundamental learning. You must know enough to know when AI is wrong. |
| :---- |

# **2.5  Student Decision Framework: Should I Use AI for This?**

Use the following decision framework whenever you are uncertain about whether or how to use AI for a task.

| Question to Ask Yourself | If the Answer Is... |
| :---- | :---- |
| 1\. Has my instructor explicitly permitted or restricted AI for this task? | Restricted: Do not use AI for that element. Permitted: Proceed with remaining questions. Silent: Apply this framework and the default Minerva policy. |
| 2\. Is the material I want to input confidential, proprietary, or private? | Yes: Do not input it into a public AI system.  No: Proceed. |
| 3\. Does using AI for this task reduce my learning for this assignment? | Yes: This use is risky or prohibited. Engage with the task independently first.  No: Proceed. |
| 4\. Will I be able to fully understand, explain, and stand behind every part of the resulting work? | No: You cannot submit work you do not understand.  Yes: Proceed. |
| 5\. Am I prepared to fully disclose this AI use in the required format? | No: Do not use AI in this way.  Yes: Proceed. |
| 6\. Does this use align with Minerva's core values (accountability, transparency, human-centered learning)? | No: Reconsider.  Yes: You may proceed with this use. |

When in doubt: Use less AI, not more. Ask your instructor. Disclose more, not less.

# **2.6  Internship and External Project Guardrails**

Internship and external project contexts introduce additional complexity because students operate simultaneously under Minerva's academic policy and the professional norms of an external organization. The following guardrails apply specifically to these contexts.

* Before your internship begins: Ask your supervisor or HR contact whether the organization has an AI policy and whether specific AI tools are approved for work deliverables.

* Assume employer data is confidential: Unless explicitly told otherwise, treat all employer information, client data, and project materials as confidential. Do not input these to any AI system without explicit employer permission.

* Disclose AI use to employers: Apply the same transparency standard to professional work as academic work. If you used AI to assist with a deliverable, disclose this to your supervisor as your professional norms require.

* IP defaults to your employer: Work product created during an internship using employer resources or time typically belongs to the employer. AI-assisted work is not exempt from this principle.

* When employer AI policy conflicts with Minerva norms: If an employer requires AI use that would violate Minerva's academic standards, consult your Minerva advisor. These situations require human judgment and institutional support.

* Avoid blending: Do not use the same AI chat session for both coursework and internship deliverables. Keep contexts cleanly separated to protect confidentiality.

# **2.7  Violations and Consequences**

Violations of this framework are treated as violations of the Minerva University Honor Code. Consequences follow the existing Academic Standards Committee (ASC) process and may include:

* Grade reductions on the affected work

* Resubmission requirements

* Formal referral to the Academic Standards Committee

* Academic probation in cases of serious or repeated violations

The severity of consequences scales with the nature of the violation. Failure to disclose AI use is treated differently from deliberately submitting AI-generated work as entirely original. In both cases, however, the honor code has been violated. Students are encouraged to clarify any uncertainties with instructors before submission, as proactive disclosure of uncertainty is always better.

**PART 3: Implementation within Minerva Forum**

# **3.1  Implementation Philosophy**

Policy documents are most effective when they are integrated into the workflows where decisions are made, not stored in a separate location that requires deliberate navigation. Minerva Forum, the platform where students receive assignments, complete coursework, and interact with instructors, is the correct implementation environment for student AI guardrails.

The implementation architecture proposed here follows a progressive disclosure model: students encounter the minimum policy guidance relevant to their current task at the moment they need it, with clear pathways to deeper guidance for complex situations. This model treats students' time and attention as limited and valuable resources.

# **3.2  Forum Architecture: AI Use Hub**

Establish a persistent AI Use Hub within Forum, accessible from:

* Every course homepage as a pinned resource

* Every assignment page as a contextual link

* The student resources sidebar

The AI Use Hub is the central repository for all student AI policy and guidance. It replaces the MyMinerva location as the primary policy home, though the MyMinerva policy page should remain and link to the Forum Hub.

## **3.2.1  Hub Architecture**

| Hub Section | Content |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Quick Decision Guide** | Interactive "Should I use AI for this?" decision tree. Takes under 2 minutes. Covers the most common student scenarios. |
| **Coursework** | Context-specific guidance for assignments, papers, coding, research, and class participation. Includes the disclosure template. |
| **Research** | Guidance for using AI in literature review, data analysis, and academic writing. Includes source verification guidance. |
| **Internships & Projects** | Guardrails for professional contexts: confidentiality, employer data, disclosure norms, IP basics. |
| **Disclosure Templates** | Pre-written, fillable Acknowledgments templates for common use cases (general, coding, creative, visual). |
| **Data Privacy Guide** | Which AI tools have which data protections. How to check AI tool privacy settings. What not to input. |
| **FAQs** | The 20 most common student questions about AI use, answered clearly and specifically. |
| **Full Policy** | The complete AI Guardrails Framework for Students in a downloadable format. |
| **Get Help** | Contact information for instructors, the Writing Studio, and the Minerva AI policy contact. |

# **3.3  Quick Decision Guide: Interactive Format**

The Quick Decision Guide should be a concise, interactive flowchart embedded in Forum. Below is the decision logic for the guide's core flow:

| Step 1 Has your instructor explicitly addressed AI use for this assignment?  YES — I am restricted: Stop. Do not use AI for the restricted element.  YES — I am permitted: Skip to Step 3\.  NO — Not mentioned: Continue to Step 2\. |
| :---- |

| Step 2 Do you have a specific, limited task in mind (brainstorming, editing, coding help, tutoring)?  YES: Continue to Step 3\.  NO (you are thinking of having AI write or complete the work): Stop. This is not a permitted use. |
| :---- |

| Step 3 Does the task involve any confidential, proprietary, or private information?  YES: Do not use a public AI system. Contact your instructor if needed.  NO: Continue to Step 4\. |
| :---- |

| Step 4 Will you be able to fully understand, verify, and stand behind all submitted content?  YES: Continue to Step 5\.  NO: Use AI differently or not at all for this task. |
| :---- |

| Step 5 Are you prepared to disclose this use fully in the required Acknowledgments format?  YES: You may proceed. Use the Disclosure Template.  NO: Do not use AI in this way. |
| :---- |

# **3.4  Instructor Integration**

Faculty should be supported and encouraged to integrate AI policy guidance directly within assignment instructions in Forum. Recommended practices:

* Include a clear AI Use Statement in every assignment: one of three labels — Permitted (with scope), Restricted (specify what is restricted and why), or Custom (link to course-specific AI policy).

* Link to the relevant Hub section from each assignment (e.g., a research assignment links to the Research section; a coding assignment links to the Coursework/Coding section).

* Specify the disclosure format expected: acknowledgments appendix, footnote, or course-specific format.

* For courses with distinctive AI policies, publish a one-page AI Policy Sheet at the start of the term and link it from every assignment.

Faculty development support should include a template for the AI Use Statement and a short guide to integrating policy links in Forum, reducing the burden on faculty while increasing student awareness.
